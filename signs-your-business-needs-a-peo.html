5 Signs Your Business Is Ready for a PEO

By Peter Sustr, National PEO Solutions Leader · IMA Financial Group · April 2026 · 6 min read

Most business owners discover PEOs one of two ways: a compliance issue forces the conversation, or a competitor starts offering better benefits and people start leaving for it.

Neither is a great way to start. By the time a problem forces the decision, you are evaluating under pressure instead of on your own terms.

Here are the five signals that tell you it is time to seriously look at a PEO — before the expensive version of that conversation finds you.

01

Someone who should not be doing HR is doing HR

In most growing businesses, HR administration falls to the owner, the office manager, or whoever happens to be organized enough to handle it. Processing payroll, managing benefits enrollment, handling workers comp paperwork, staying current on employment law changes — none of this is someone's actual job.

The cost is real even if it does not show up on a balance sheet. Calculate how many hours per month go into HR-adjacent tasks and multiply by what that person's time is worth. For most companies in the 10-50 employee range, the number is $2,000-$5,000 per month in misallocated labor — before accounting for the risk of something being done wrong.

A PEO does not just take those tasks off the plate. It professionalizes them. Payroll does not run late. Tax filings do not get missed. Benefits enrollment does not happen in a spreadsheet.

02

You are losing candidates to competitors with better benefits

Small businesses consistently cite benefits as one of the top reasons they lose hiring decisions to larger competitors. A candidate with two comparable offers will frequently choose the one with better health insurance, a stronger 401(k) match, or more ancillary coverage — even at a lower base salary.

On the open market, a 20-person company cannot access the same health plan rates as a 2,000-person company. That is not a negotiating failure — it is simple math. Insurance carriers price based on risk pool size.

A PEO pools your employees with tens of thousands of others across their client base. Your 20 employees get large-group pricing. The difference in plan quality and premium rates can be significant — often enough to compete with employers five or ten times your size.

If you have lost a hire in the last 12 months partially because of benefits, that is the signal.

03

Workers comp is eating into your margins

Workers compensation insurance is one of the most reliably expensive line items for businesses with any physical component to their workforce — construction, healthcare, manufacturing, food service, distribution. Rates compound over time as your experience modification factor (X-mod) reflects your claims history.

PEOs underwrite workers comp through their master policy. The rates your employees are covered under reflect the PEO's aggregate claims experience across thousands of companies — not just yours. For businesses in higher-risk industries or those who have had claims, the savings can be 15% to 40% on workers comp costs alone.

Beyond cost, PEOs also provide claims management and return-to-work programs that reduce the frequency and severity of future claims. This is one of the clearest ROI calculations in the PEO evaluation.

04

You are hiring in multiple states

The moment you hire an employee in a second state, your compliance picture doubles. Each state has different payroll tax registration requirements, different unemployment insurance rates, different leave laws, and different employer regulations. Add a third state and it gets meaningfully more complex.

Most small businesses handle multi-state compliance one of two ways: they pay a lawyer or HR consultant to stay current, or they do not stay current and hope for the best. Neither is a great long-term strategy.

PEOs handle multi-state compliance routinely. They maintain registrations in all 50 states, track employment law changes proactively, and advise you when something in your operating states requires attention. For remote-first companies or businesses in growth mode, this is increasingly one of the clearest reasons to evaluate a PEO.

05

An employment issue has made you realize how exposed you are

A termination dispute. A wage and hour complaint. An employee claiming they were not properly classified. A manager who should not have been left unsupported in a performance conversation.

These situations happen to good companies. What separates companies that handle them well from companies that do not is usually whether they had HR infrastructure and Employment Practices Liability Insurance (EPLI) coverage in place before the issue arose.

Most PEO arrangements include EPLI coverage as part of the package. They also provide HR advisory support — a real HR professional you can call before you make a decision, not after the complaint has been filed.

If you have had an employment issue in the past two years, or if you have a situation in your business that you know is unresolved and could become one, that is the signal to act now rather than later.

What if some of these apply but you are not sure?

That is exactly what the assessment conversation is for. A good PEO advisor will tell you honestly whether the signals you are seeing add up to a genuine PEO opportunity — and if they do not, they will tell you that too.

There is no obligation to move forward from a conversation. The only cost of getting an honest outside assessment is 20 minutes.

One thing worth knowing: Using an independent PEO advisor costs employers nothing. Advisors are compensated by the PEO you ultimately choose, at a rate already built into standard pricing. A good advisor brings market benchmarks, comparison across multiple providers, and negotiating leverage — none of which you get by calling a PEO directly.
