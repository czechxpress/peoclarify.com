How to Choose a PEO: 8 Questions Every Business Owner Should Ask

By Peter Sustr, National PEO Solutions Leader · IMA Financial Group · April 2026 · 9 min read

Most business owners evaluate a PEO the same way: they call one or two of the most recognizable names, sit through sales presentations, and choose the one that seemed least aggressive. That is not an evaluation. That is a coin flip with extra steps.

The difference between a good PEO fit and a poor one shows up 12 to 18 months after implementation — in service quality, benefits renewal pricing, and whether the relationship delivers what was promised. By then, switching costs are real.

These eight questions cut through the presentation and tell you what you actually need to know before you sign anything.

Question 1

Are you IRS-certified (CPEO) — and does that matter for my situation?

A Certified Professional Employer Organization (CPEO) has been vetted by the IRS and meets specific financial and operational standards. CPEO certification matters for two reasons: it affects how certain payroll tax liabilities are handled at year-end, and it signals a baseline of financial stability and operational discipline.

Not every PEO pursues CPEO status — it is expensive to obtain and maintain. But if you are evaluating a non-certified provider, you should understand specifically why they chose not to certify and what protections you have as a result.

What you're listening forA clear yes with the certification number, or a specific and credible explanation for why certification was not pursued. Vague answers here are a yellow flag.
Question 2

Who exactly will be handling my account day-to-day — and what is their caseload?

The single biggest predictor of PEO satisfaction is not the technology platform or the benefits package. It is service quality — specifically, whether there is a real person assigned to your account who knows your business and picks up the phone.

Large PEOs often present during the sales process with senior people who have excellent answers. After implementation, you may end up in a shared service queue with a different team entirely. Ask specifically: who will be your named account manager, what is their title and tenure, and how many clients do they manage?

Industry best practice is one HR Business Partner per 30-50 client companies. If the answer is 100+ clients per advisor, expect service to reflect that math.

What you're listening forA specific name, title, and honest answer about caseload. If they cannot tell you who your day-to-day contact will be before you sign, that tells you something.
Question 3

What health insurance carriers are in your network — and what are the actual plan options?

Benefits quality is one of the primary reasons businesses join a PEO. But "access to health insurance" means nothing without knowing which carriers, which networks, and which plan designs are actually available in your state.

Ask to see the actual plan options — not a summary — for your specific employee zip codes. Carrier networks vary dramatically by geography. A Blue Cross or Aetna plan with a strong network in Denver may have a thin network in a rural market. Request the provider directory for the plan options you are being shown.

Also ask: what happens to renewal pricing? PEOs often quote attractive first-year rates. Find out whether the year-two renewal is market-rated or whether it reflects your specific claims experience. The answer materially affects the long-term economics.

What you're listening forSpecific carrier names for your geography, willingness to show you actual plan documents, and honest disclosure of how renewal pricing works.
Question 4

How is workers comp handled — and what is my rate based on?

PEOs cover workers comp under their master policy, which is one of the most significant potential cost advantages of a PEO arrangement. But not all PEOs underwrite workers comp equally, and not all risk classes are welcome on all master policies.

Ask specifically: what workers comp carrier is the master policy with? What rate per $100 of payroll are you quoting for my class codes? Is that rate based on the PEO's aggregate claims experience or my specific claims history? And what happens to my rate if I have a claim?

For businesses in higher-risk industries — construction, healthcare, manufacturing, food service — this question can be the single most important financial consideration in the PEO evaluation.

What you're listening forSpecific carrier name, specific rate per $100 of payroll for your class codes, clear explanation of how claims affect future pricing.
Question 5

What does your all-in pricing include — and what are the add-ons?

PEO proposals frequently lead with an attractive headline rate and then add fees for onboarding, off-cycle payroll runs, benefits administration, compliance filings, or technology modules. By the time implementation is complete, the effective per-employee cost can be 20-40% higher than the quoted PEPM.

Ask for a complete fee schedule in writing — every charge you could possibly incur in a normal operating year. Then ask: what is not included in this list that you have charged other clients for? A good PEO will answer that question directly.

What you're listening forA written, comprehensive fee schedule with no significant items described as "situational" or "case by case."
Question 6

What states do you have experience operating in — and do you have clients in my industry?

A PEO that runs payroll and HR for professional services firms in five states may not have deep expertise in construction compliance or healthcare staffing regulations. Industry-specific knowledge matters more than most prospects realize during the evaluation.

Ask for client references in your industry and in the states where you operate. Then call them. Specifically ask: what surprised you about working with this PEO — good or bad — that you wish you had known before you signed?

What you're listening forGenuine industry-specific examples, not generic references. Reluctance to provide references in your industry or geography is meaningful information.
Question 7

What does the exit process look like?

This is the question almost nobody asks — and the one that causes the most pain when the relationship does not work out.

When you leave a PEO, you need to move your employees to a new benefits carrier, establish new workers comp coverage, set up a new payroll system, and transfer employee data. How the PEO facilitates — or obstructs — that process determines how disruptive the transition is.

Ask specifically: what is the notice period required to terminate? What data do you provide upon exit and in what format? Do you assist with benefits continuity during transition? Are there exit fees?

A PEO that answers this question clearly and confidently is telling you something important about how they operate. A PEO that is evasive is telling you something equally important.

What you're listening forClear, specific answers about notice period, data portability, and transition support. Get it in writing before you sign.
Question 8

How do you handle employment practices issues — and what does your EPLI coverage actually protect?

Employment Practices Liability Insurance (EPLI) is one of the most valuable components of a PEO arrangement — and one of the least understood. It covers claims related to wrongful termination, discrimination, harassment, and wage and hour disputes.

Ask specifically: what is the coverage limit on the EPLI policy? What is excluded from coverage? Is the coverage a blanket policy or does it require separate underwriting for my specific risk profile? And critically: in the event of a claim, does the PEO's HR team support me through the process or does it go directly to insurance?

A well-structured PEO relationship means HR advisory involvement before a situation escalates to a claim — not just coverage after the fact.

What you're listening forSpecific coverage limits, clear description of exclusions, and confirmation that HR advisory support is available before you are in a claim situation — not only after.

One more thing: the comparison problem

These eight questions are valuable. But they only help you if you ask them of multiple providers and have a benchmark to evaluate the answers against.

Most business owners do not have that benchmark. They have heard one or two answers to these questions and have nothing to compare them to. This is why PEO selection consistently produces outcomes that look good on day one and mediocre by year two.

The case for an independent advisor: A PEO advisor who has evaluated hundreds of proposals can tell you within minutes whether a specific answer is market standard, favorable, or concerning. That context — which you cannot get by talking to PEOs directly — is what turns these eight questions from interesting to actionable.
